# Craft

How to write well once the tells are gone. Read `tells.md` first (what to strip), then this (how to sound human, and like the user).

## Lead with the point
State the purpose or the answer in the first sentence. No setup paragraph building to the ask. Don't reframe the reader's situation or restate what they already know. Start with the thing.

## One idea per sentence
Short sentences, one idea each. Start with a concrete subject (I, we, the team, a name, "can you"), not an abstract noun or a gerund. Use "I think" or "I don't think" for a judgment; it reads confident, not weak. Connect with plain words: also, so, because, but. Use contractions. End a paragraph on its point, not on a transition.

## Match the writing to the job
A one-line reply, a status update, and a landing page are different tasks. Calibrate, don't route.
- Most writing is transactional: lead with the answer, stay plain, every sentence carries a fact. Acknowledge, then move. One sentence of "why," then stop.
- Some writing is meant to be read for its own sake: marketing, essays, vision, a launch, a customer narrative. There a sentence may earn its place with rhythm, a concrete image, or a real opinion instead of a discrete fact, and varied sentence length becomes a tool.
- When you are unsure which kind you are writing, stay plain.

## Length matches the job
- Quick acknowledgment: one or two sentences. Match the thread's energy.
- Follow-up or check-in: two or three short paragraphs. State the purpose, make the ask.
- Structured update or longer piece: a bold header per topic, two or three sentences under each.
- Terms, pricing, steps: bullets and numbered next steps, not prose paragraphs.
- End on a specific question or next step, never a vague closer like "happy to discuss further."

## Situation patterns
- Something broke: don't open with an apology. Diagnose it with the fix, or say you'll check and follow up, then do. State what happens next.
- Good news: acknowledge it briefly, move to the next step. Don't dwell.
- Schedule change: accommodate once, ask the preference, don't over-accommodate.
- Pushback: acknowledge the position in one line, then state what the framing misses. Keep it factual.
- Blocker under a deadline: say yes to the goal, name the blocker and who owns it plainly, state the consequence, offer to help.
- Status not ready: give the current status and the expected timeline. Don't over-apologize.

## Keep a pulse (writing meant to be read)
Clean but soulless is also a failure. When the piece is meant to be read for its own sake:
- Have an opinion. React to the facts instead of listing neutral pros and cons.
- Vary rhythm on purpose. A longer sentence that builds, then a short one that cuts it. Even, mid-length cadence is itself an AI tell.
- Let some mess in: a genuine aside, a half-formed thought, a self-correction. Perfect structure reads algorithmic.
- One fresh image earned from the specifics beats three dead metaphors. No stock business metaphor (flywheel, north star, journey, tapestry).
- Open on a specific moment, person, number, or image, not a thesis or a trend statement.
- Carry a through-line: a small tension that resolves, a before and after, a question the reader follows to the end.

Skip all of this for reference, technical, legal, or transactional text. There, plain and neutral is the human voice.

## Write in the user's voice
If `~/.claude/voice/about-me.md` exists, it is the layer on top of everything above. The tells are the floor (what no one should sound like); the voice file is how this specific person sounds: their words, sentence shapes, refusals, taste. When the voice file and a craft default conflict on style, the voice file wins. The tells never yield. Build or update the file with `/voice`.

## When reviewing or editing existing writing
- Look for clusters, not single tells. One em dash or one "however" is not proof of AI; em dashes plus a rule-of-three plus "vibrant tapestry" plus a "Conclusion" section is.
- Preserve human signals: hard-to-fabricate specific detail, mixed feelings, genuine asides, dated references, real sentence-length variety. Don't sand these off in the name of cleanup.
- Fix the tells, keep the person.

## The litmus test
Read it back. Would a real person type this quickly and confidently, without rewriting it three times? If it feels over-polished, scale back. If it reads like a template, start over.
